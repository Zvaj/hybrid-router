# Applied Ethics

## Corporate Ethics and Accountability

Corporate Responsibility encompasses the obligations organizations owe to employees, customers, communities, and the environment. ESG Metrics — environmental, social, and governance indicators — give investors and regulators tools to assess whether companies are meeting those obligations. Sustainability Reporting makes performance public; Greenwashing is the practice of publishing misleading reports to appear more responsible than actual practices warrant. Ethics Washing is the analogous practice of adopting ethical language without substantive change.

Corporate Accountability mechanisms range from internal Whistleblowing protections to external Regulatory Enforcement and Legal Liability. The B Corp Certification and Triple Bottom Line frameworks institutionalize accountability by requiring companies to weigh profit against social and environmental outcomes. Supply Chain Ethics extends accountability upstream: a company is responsible for labor conditions in its suppliers, not just its own operations. Due Diligence is the process of actually investigating those conditions rather than accepting self-reported assurances.

## Harm Prevention and Advocacy

Advocacy Strategies convert ethical analysis into change. Policy Design requires understanding which interventions are feasible and effective — market-based mechanisms like Carbon Pricing and Pigouvian Taxes change incentives without mandating behavior; Regulatory Approaches set floors through legal requirements; Subsidies reward desired behavior rather than punishing undesired behavior. Incentive Design synthesizes these tools.

Social Impact Assessment evaluates what a proposed policy, product, or corporate practice will actually do to communities. This requires the same rigorous Quantitative Analysis and Qualitative Analysis applied in harm research. Stakeholder Engagement brings affected parties into the assessment process rather than relying solely on expert judgment. The Intervention Hierarchy, drawing on Donella Meadows's work on Leverage Points, ranks interventions from Low Leverage Points (adjusting parameters) to High Leverage Points (changing system goals and paradigm shifts).

## Data-Driven Ethics in Practice

Data-Driven Ethics applies the tools of empirical research to moral questions. Algorithmic bias in hiring, lending, and criminal justice systems produces measurable disparate impact across demographic groups. Identifying this impact requires Ethical Data Collection with appropriate Sampling Methods — Selection Bias in data collection produces misleading results that downstream systems amplify. Anonymization and Data Privacy protections are prerequisites for collecting sensitive data ethically.

The Harm Per Revenue metric and similar Harm Scorecards allow cross-industry comparison: how much harm does each dollar of revenue in tobacco, fossil fuels, and ultra-processed foods produce? Industry Comparisons using Normalized Metrics reveal which sectors produce the most harm per unit of economic activity. Multi-Dimensional Harm analysis accounts for Cumulative Impact and Intergenerational Harm that single-metric analyses miss.

## Systems Thinking and Ethics

Complex ethical problems — climate change, addiction, institutional corruption — are Systems Thinking problems. Feedback Loops create dynamics that simple linear reasoning misses: a Reinforcing Loop in the attention economy amplifies engagement regardless of user wellbeing; a Balancing Loop in regulatory systems tends to restore equilibrium after disruption. Causal Loop Diagrams make these dynamics visible and debatable.

Tipping Points and Thresholds are where systems change character abruptly — a carbon concentration that triggers runaway feedback, a social norm that tips from minority to majority practice. Ethical action often requires intervention before a threshold is crossed, because post-threshold reversal may be impossible. System Archetypes like Shifting the Burden and Tragedy of the Commons identify recurring structural patterns that produce predictable ethical failures.
