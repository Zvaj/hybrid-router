# Ethical Frameworks

## Traditional Moral Philosophy

Traditional Moral Philosophy provides three foundational lenses for Ethical Reasoning. Consequentialism evaluates actions entirely by outcomes: the right choice maximizes aggregate welfare. This framework appears throughout Evidence-Based Ethics — when policymakers use cost-benefit analysis to compare interventions, they are reasoning consequentially. Deontology, associated with Immanuel Kant, holds that certain duties bind regardless of consequences. Lying is wrong even when it produces better outcomes; harming one person to save five violates a duty not to use people as means. Virtue ethics shifts the question from "what should I do?" to "what kind of agent should I be?" — emphasizing character traits like honesty, courage, and practical wisdom.

No single framework captures the full complexity of real decisions. Data-Driven Ethics draws on all three: it measures outcomes consequentially, respects rights and duties deontologically, and asks what ethically excellent institutions look like in virtue-ethics terms. Critical Thinking and Analytical Writing are the tools that prevent any single framework from becoming dogma.

## Applying Frameworks to Technology

Technology creates novel ethical terrain that each framework addresses differently. A consequentialist asks what aggregate harm or benefit a surveillance system produces — accounting for security benefits and privacy costs at scale. A deontologist insists that informed consent is required regardless of whether consent changes the outcome. A virtue ethicist asks whether an organization collecting data treats users as partners or as resources.

The Scientific Method, applied to ethics, means testing normative claims against empirical evidence. Statistical Thinking and Quantitative Analysis make abstract philosophical questions concrete: instead of debating whether algorithmic hiring is fair in the abstract, measure disparate impact across demographic groups and compare it against a baseline. Objectivity and Subjectivity both matter — researchers must acknowledge where Bias Recognition fails and Cognitive Biases distort judgment.

## Consequentialism in Practice

Consequentialist reasoning requires measuring outcomes, which leads directly to the tools of Harm Quantification. Mortality Rate and Morbidity Rate are the basic unit-denominators; DALYs (disability-adjusted life years) combine them into a single comparable metric. Social Cost Accounting extends consequences beyond direct health effects to economic and environmental impacts. Cost-Benefit Analysis weighs interventions against each other using commensurable units.

Confirmation Bias is the most dangerous failure mode for consequentialist reasoning: decision-makers seek evidence that confirms their preferred policy and discount evidence that contradicts it. Rigorous Research Methods — Systematic Review, Meta-Analysis, Independent Verification — exist specifically to counteract this tendency. Source Triangulation, combining Primary Sources, Secondary Sources, and Academic Sources, reduces dependence on any single line of evidence.

## Deontology and Rights

Deontological constraints set floors beneath which no consequentialist calculation can push behavior. Informed Consent is a deontological requirement: research subjects must agree to how their data is used, regardless of the benefits to society. Data Privacy protects autonomy even when sharing data would improve aggregate outcomes. Whistleblowing is framed deontologically — individuals have a duty to report wrongdoing even when doing so produces personal costs.

Corporate Responsibility applies deontological thinking to institutions: corporations owe duties to employees, customers, and communities that exist independently of profit maximization. ESG Metrics attempt to operationalize these duties in ways that can be tracked and reported.
