# Introduction to Ethics

## What Ethics Is

Ethics is the systematic study of moral principles that govern how individuals and institutions distinguish right from wrong, and how they ought to act. It is not simply a catalogue of rules but an ongoing inquiry into the foundations of human conduct. Philosophers draw a useful distinction between descriptive ethics — which observes and reports what people actually believe and do — and normative ethics, which asks what people should believe and do. This course focuses on normative ethics while drawing heavily on data, evidence, and empirical research to ground abstract principles in measurable reality. This approach, called data-driven ethics, treats ethical reasoning as continuous with scientific method and critical thinking rather than separate from it.

## Traditional Moral Philosophy

Three frameworks from traditional moral philosophy have shaped ethical reasoning for centuries. Consequentialism holds that the morality of an action is determined entirely by its outcomes — the right choice is the one that produces the greatest good for the greatest number. Deontology, associated with Immanuel Kant, argues that certain duties and rules bind us regardless of consequences: lying is wrong even when it might produce a better outcome. Virtue ethics shifts focus from actions to character, asking not "what should I do?" but "what kind of person should I be?"

Consider a concrete dilemma: a company discovers a product defect that poses moderate risk to some users, but issuing a recall will cause significant financial harm to employees. A consequentialist weighs aggregate outcomes — lives protected versus livelihoods lost. A deontologist insists on a duty to disclose harm to customers regardless of cost. A virtue ethicist asks what an honest, courageous, and responsible organization would do. Ethical reasoning draws on all three frameworks together, since no single lens captures the full complexity of real decisions.

## Data-Driven Ethics and Harm

Data-driven ethics extends these traditional frameworks into the digital age, where algorithms, surveillance systems, and large-scale data collection create novel forms of harm. Algorithmic bias can reproduce and amplify existing inequalities in lending, hiring, and criminal justice. Privacy violations erode autonomy in ways that no single actor intends. Harm quantification — measuring harm using tools like mortality rates, morbidity rates, and DALYs (disability-adjusted life years) — makes abstract ethical judgments tractable and comparable across populations.

Harm recognition is the first step toward advocacy. Once harm is identified and quantified through rigorous research methods and source triangulation, ethical reasoning demands a response: individuals, communities, and institutions must act to reduce it. Bias recognition and awareness of cognitive biases like confirmation bias are essential tools for this work.

## Corporate Ethics

Organizations face ethical responsibilities toward employees, customers, and communities simultaneously. Corporate ethics is not merely about avoiding scandal — it requires embedding ethical reasoning into product decisions, data practices, and stakeholder relationships. Organizations that apply evidence-based ethics systematically, rather than reactively, build more durable trust and produce fewer harms at scale.
